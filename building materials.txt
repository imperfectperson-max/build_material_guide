5. Literature Review
5.1 Machine Learning in Construction Price Forecasting
The application of machine learning to construction material price forecasting has gained significant traction in recent years. A comprehensive systematic literature review analyzing publications from 2012 to 2022 identified that Vector Error Correction Model (VECM), Artificial Neural Networks (ANN), and Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) were the most frequently employed forecasting models. The review noted a significant increase in research activity since 2016, indicating growing interest in applying advanced analytical techniques to construction economics.
Research demonstrates that forecasting models can be broadly classified into causal modeling and time-series analysis categories. Causal models analyze historical data alongside independent variables such as Consumer Price Index (CPI), Producer Price Index (PPI), and Gross Domestic Product (GDP) to forecast construction material prices. Time-series models, conversely, rely primarily on historical price patterns to predict future values.
5.2 Comparative Model Performance
Recent comparative studies have provided valuable insights into relative model performance. A 2025 framework for construction material price forecasting demonstrated that incorporating explanatory variables significantly improves predictive performance across all model types. The study found that LSTM models consistently achieved the highest accuracy, with improvements of up to 59% over traditional ARIMA models.
Research comparing XGBoost, Random Forest, and LSTM for time series forecasting revealed that each model has distinct advantages. XGBoost performs well at finding complex relationships between variables and includes regularization features that help avoid overfitting. Random Forest, as an ensemble method combining multiple decision trees, provides robust predictions across varied datasets. LSTM networks excel at capturing time-related patterns through their sophisticated gating mechanism, making them particularly effective for sequential data with long-term dependencies.
A comparative analysis examining machine learning methods for forecasting in different contexts found that Random Forest with differentiated inputs proved to be the best performing method across scenarios with varying complexity, including situations with jumps, random walks, or combinations of both. The study noted that while XGBoost was competitive, machine learning algorithms generally delivered consistently better results than traditional time series methods when additional complexities were present.
